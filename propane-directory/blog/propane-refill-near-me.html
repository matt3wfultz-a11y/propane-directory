Propane Refill Near Me: How to Find the Cheapest Station in 2026

May 1, 2026

If you're searching for a propane refill near you, the good news is you probably have more options than you think — and most of them are cheaper than the exchange cages outside your local big-box store. This guide covers exactly where to look, what you'll pay by tank size, and the fastest ways to find a refill station near you right now.

Where to Find a Propane Refill Station Near You

Propane refill stations come in several varieties. Knowing which type to search for helps you find one faster:

  • Dedicated propane dealers: Local suppliers like Ferrellgas, AmeriGas bulk dealers, and independent propane companies offer the lowest per-gallon prices. They fill tanks of all sizes — 20 lb, 33 lb, 100 lb, and up. Search Google Maps for "propane refill" or "propane dealer" near your zip code to find these.
  • U-Haul locations: U-Haul is one of the most consistently available propane refill options across the country. Most U-Haul centers fill by weight and accept 20-lb and 33-lb cylinders. Use the U-Haul store finder and filter by "propane" to locate the nearest one.
  • Tractor Supply Co.: Thousands of Tractor Supply locations nationwide offer propane refill at an outdoor fill station. Prices are competitive and staff are trained to fill by weight. Their store locator lets you filter by "propane refill."
  • Hardware stores: Some Ace Hardware and True Value locations offer refill service. Availability varies by store — call ahead.
  • RV dealers and campgrounds: If you need a refill for a larger cylinder (40 lb, 100 lb) or an RV's built-in tank, RV service centers along major highways often have fill stations.
  • Gas stations and convenience stores: A smaller number of gas stations and truck stops carry propane refill service, especially on rural routes.

Refill vs Exchange: Why Refill Is Almost Always Cheaper

The propane exchange cages outside Walmart, Home Depot, Kroger, and most grocery stores are convenient — but they cost significantly more per gallon, and they give you less gas.

OptionGas You GetTypical CostCost Per Gallon
Refill (local dealer / U-Haul / Tractor Supply)~20 lbs / 4.7 gal (full)$15–$22$3.20–$4.70
Exchange (Blue Rhino / AmeriGas cage)~15 lbs / 3.5 gal only$24–$28$4.80–$5.60

Exchange programs fill tanks to only 15 lbs, not the full 20-lb capacity — and charge you more per gallon for that partial fill. On a single tank swap, you typically save $7–$12 by refilling instead of exchanging. Over a grilling season or a camping trip, that gap adds up fast.

The main case for exchange: your tank is too old to refill (tanks expire 12 years after manufacture), you need a cylinder immediately and there's no refill station nearby, or you want to return a damaged tank. For everything else, refill wins on cost.

Before You Go: What to Check

  • Check the collar stamp: Look for a date stamped on the collar of your cylinder — for example, "06 14" means the tank was manufactured in June 2014. Most tanks must be recertified after 12 years. If your tank is past its date, a refill station may refuse to fill it. See our guide on propane tank recertification for your options.
  • Inspect the valve and threads: A visibly corroded or damaged OPD valve may cause a dealer to decline the fill. Check for obvious damage before driving over.
  • Transport safely: Stand the cylinder upright in your vehicle. If you're transporting in an enclosed car, crack a window. Never leave a propane cylinder in a hot, sealed car.
  • Bring payment: Most refill stations accept both cash and card, but call ahead if you're unsure.

How Much to Fill: Full vs Partial Refill

Good refill stations fill by weight, not by a fixed amount. Bring your tank at any level — empty, half-empty, or nearly full — and you'll pay only for the propane added. There's no penalty for partial fills. If you want to top off a tank that's still a quarter full, you can do that.

Filling to 80% capacity (the legal maximum) is standard practice. A 20-lb cylinder filled to 80% holds about 4.7 gallons. Stations that fill to 100% are overfilling, which is both illegal and dangerous — avoid them.
